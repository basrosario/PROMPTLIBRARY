AI Safety & Ethics

Focus: Critical Evaluation Goal: Informed AI Use Audience: Everyone

AI is powerful, but it's not infallible. This guide helps you recognize AI-generated misinformation, spot deepfakes, evaluate AI outputs critically, and use AI tools responsibly. In an era where synthetic content is increasingly sophisticated, digital literacy is essential.

Trust But Verify

AI can generate convincing content that is completely false. The most important skill in the AI age isn't using AI—it's knowing when NOT to trust it.

Recognizing Deepfakes

Deepfakes are AI-generated videos, images, or audio that make people appear to say or do things they never did.

Video Deepfake Signs
  • Unnatural blinking patterns or no blinking
  • Lighting inconsistencies on face vs. background
  • Blurry or distorted areas around hairline, ears, or jewelry
  • Lip movements that don't quite sync with audio
  • Skin texture that appears too smooth or waxy
  • Teeth that look blurred or inconsistent
Audio Deepfake Signs
  • Robotic or unnatural speech patterns
  • Breathing sounds that don't match speech rhythm
  • Inconsistent background noise
  • Unusual pauses or cadence
  • Emotional tone that doesn't match content
  • Mispronunciations of familiar words or names
Image Deepfake Signs
  • Asymmetrical facial features
  • Inconsistent earrings, glasses, or accessories
  • Warped backgrounds near face edges
  • Text or logos that appear distorted
  • Fingers with extra or missing joints
  • Hair that melts into the background
Verification Steps
  • Reverse image search the source
  • Check if the original source is reputable
  • Look for the same content from multiple sources
  • Use deepfake detection tools when available
  • Check metadata if accessible
  • Ask: "Who benefits from this being true?"
Real-World Impact: In 2024, a finance worker in Hong Kong transferred $25 million after a deepfake video call with what appeared to be the company's CFO. The entire call—multiple executives—was AI-generated. Always verify through secondary channels for any high-stakes requests.

AI-Generated Misinformation

AI can generate completely fabricated information that sounds authoritative and confident. This is called "hallucination."

Common AI Hallucination Types
  • Fake Citations: AI invents academic papers, books, and authors that don't exist. The citations look perfectly formatted but lead nowhere.
  • Fabricated Statistics: AI generates specific percentages and numbers without any actual data source. "Studies show 73% of..." may be completely made up.
  • Invented Quotes: AI attributes statements to real people that they never said. The quote sounds plausible but is fictional.
  • False Historical Events: AI may describe events that never happened or mix up details from different events.
  • Non-Existent Products/Companies: AI can describe features of products or companies that don't exist as if they're real.
  • Confident Incorrectness: AI states wrong information with the same confidence as correct information. Tone is not a reliability indicator.
Fact-Check AI OutputCRISP
[Context] I received information from an AI system and need to verify its accuracy before using it in my work.
[Request] Help me fact-check the following AI-generated content: [Paste content here].
[Instructions] Identify: (1) Any claims that need verification; (2) Statistics or numbers that should have sources; (3) Quotes attributed to real people; (4) References to studies, papers, or organizations; (5) Historical claims or dates.
[Style] Systematic and skeptical.
[Parameters] For each claim identified, suggest how to verify it (specific database, official source, fact-checking site). Flag any claims that cannot be easily verified.
Verification Best Practices

Never cite AI-generated sources without independent verification. Search for the exact citation in Google Scholar, library databases, or the publisher's website. If you can't find it, assume it doesn't exist.

The "Too Perfect" Warning Sign

If AI provides exactly the statistic or quote you were hoping for, be extra suspicious. AI is trained to be helpful, which means it may generate "ideal" answers rather than accurate ones.

Evaluating AI-Recommended Sources

When AI suggests websites, tools, or resources, apply the same critical evaluation you would to any recommendation.

Source Credibility CheckerCOSTAR
[Context] I was recommended a resource/website/tool by AI and want to evaluate whether it's legitimate and trustworthy before using it.
[Objective] Provide a framework for evaluating the credibility of this resource: [Name of resource/URL].
[Style] Critical evaluation framework using established criteria.
[Tone] Skeptical but fair—assume nothing, verify everything.
[Audience] Someone who wants to make an informed decision about using this resource.
[Response] Evaluate using: (1) Authority - who owns/operates this? What are their credentials? (2) Accuracy - can claims be verified elsewhere? (3) Currency - when was it last updated? (4) Purpose - what is the goal? Educational, commercial, persuasive? (5) Coverage - is the information comprehensive or selective? (6) Red Flags - pop-ups, sensational claims, no contact info, grammatical errors.
Red Flags for Fake Resources
  • No verifiable author or organization - Legitimate sources identify who created the content
  • Recently created domain - Check domain age using WHOIS lookup tools
  • No physical address or contact information - Real organizations have real locations
  • Only positive reviews - No legitimate product has 100% positive feedback
  • Pressure tactics - "Limited time!" "Act now!" "Everyone is doing this!"
  • Too good to be true claims - Free money, guaranteed results, miracle solutions
  • Poor grammar and spelling - Professional organizations proofread their content
  • No other sources reference it - Legitimate resources are cited by others

Real-World AI Incidents

Learning from documented cases helps us understand the risks and implement better safeguards.

Documented AI Failures & Lessons

Financial Sector

  • Deepfake CFO Scam (2024): A Hong Kong company lost $25 million after employees participated in a video call where every participant—including the CFO—was AI-generated. Lesson: Verify identity through secondary channels for any financial request.
  • AI Stock Recommendations: Multiple instances of AI chatbots providing investment advice that resulted in significant losses. Lesson: AI is not a licensed financial advisor and cannot be held accountable.

Legal Sector

  • Fake Case Citations (2023): A lawyer used ChatGPT to research cases and submitted a brief citing six completely fabricated court decisions. The lawyer faced sanctions. Lesson: Always verify legal citations in official databases.
  • Contract Hallucinations: AI tools have generated contract clauses that contradict themselves or include terms that are unenforceable. Lesson: Legal documents require human review by qualified professionals.

Healthcare

  • Medical Misinformation: AI chatbots have provided incorrect medication dosages, contraindicated drug combinations, and missed serious symptoms. Lesson: Never use AI as a replacement for medical professionals.
  • Diagnostic Errors: AI diagnostic tools have both over-diagnosed (causing unnecessary procedures) and under-diagnosed (missing serious conditions). Lesson: AI is a tool to assist, not replace, clinical judgment.

Education

  • Fabricated Research: Students submitting AI-generated papers with fake citations have faced academic integrity violations. Lesson: AI output requires verification; submitting unverified AI content is risky.
  • AI Detection Errors: AI detection tools have falsely flagged human-written work as AI-generated, causing unfair accusations. Lesson: Detection tools are not 100% accurate; process matters as much as product.

AI Best Practices

Use AI effectively while maintaining critical thinking and ethical standards.

Verify Before You Trust

Treat AI output as a first draft that requires fact-checking. Cross-reference any factual claims, statistics, or citations with authoritative primary sources.

Understand the Limitations

AI doesn't "know" things—it predicts likely responses based on training data. It can't access real-time information, verify its own accuracy, or learn from your corrections.

Protect Sensitive Information

Never input confidential data, personal information, or proprietary business information into public AI tools. Assume anything you share could become public.

Disclose AI Use Appropriately

Be transparent when AI contributed to your work. Many institutions and employers have policies about AI disclosure—know yours and follow them.

Maintain Human Oversight

AI should augment human decision-making, not replace it. High-stakes decisions—medical, legal, financial, safety—require qualified human judgment.

Question the Source

AI can be confidently wrong. If something sounds too perfect, too aligned with what you wanted to hear, or too specific to be true, it probably needs verification.

Critical AI EvaluationCRISPE
[Capacity] You are a critical thinking coach helping someone evaluate AI-generated content.
[Role] Skeptical analyst who questions assumptions and identifies potential issues.
[Insight] I received this output from an AI and want to evaluate it critically before using it: [Paste AI output here].
[Statement] Analyze this content for: (1) Factual claims that need verification; (2) Potential biases in framing or word choice; (3) Missing perspectives or alternative viewpoints; (4) Logical fallacies or weak arguments; (5) Confidence level appropriate to the evidence; (6) Anything that seems "too convenient" or suspiciously perfect.
[Personality] Constructively critical—not cynical, but appropriately skeptical.
[Experiment] Suggest specific questions I should ask myself before using this content.

Ethical AI Use Principles

The E.T.H.I.C.S. Framework
  • Evaluate - Always critically assess AI outputs before use
  • Transparency - Disclose AI involvement in your work when appropriate
  • Human Oversight - Keep humans in the loop for important decisions
  • Integrity - Don't use AI to deceive, manipulate, or harm others
  • Confidentiality - Protect sensitive data; don't share it with AI systems
  • Source Verification - Verify all citations, statistics, and factual claims
Remember: AI is a tool, and like any tool, its value depends on how it's used. A hammer can build a house or cause harm—the same is true of AI. Your critical thinking, ethical judgment, and verification practices are what make AI use responsible and beneficial.

Quick Reference Prompts

Verify a Citation

"I need to verify this citation: [paste citation]. Search for this exact paper/book in academic databases. If it doesn't exist, tell me clearly."

Check for Bias

"Analyze this content for potential biases: [paste content]. What perspectives might be missing? What assumptions does it make?"

Find Alternative Viewpoints

"What are the strongest counterarguments to this position: [paste position]? Present them fairly without dismissing them."

Identify Verification Steps

"What specific steps should I take to verify these claims: [paste claims]? What sources would be authoritative for each?"

Evaluate a Source

"Help me evaluate the credibility of [website/organization]. What should I look for to determine if it's trustworthy?"

Question AI Confidence

"For each claim you just made, rate your confidence level (high/medium/low) and explain what would change your confidence."

Your Best Defense: Critical Thinking

Technology changes rapidly, but critical thinking skills are timeless. The techniques on this page—questioning sources, verifying claims, seeking multiple perspectives—work for evaluating any information, whether AI-generated or not. Cultivate skepticism, practice verification, and never stop asking "How do I know this is true?"
